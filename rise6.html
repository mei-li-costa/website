6 - The Rise of the Cult of Chastity

During the Mongol conquest of the late 1270s, the Mongol rulers imposed a levirate on marriage laws. A window was told to stay with her in-laws and marry her husband’s younger brother, if possible. Women could no longer retain their dowries and return to their families. Remaining chaste then became the only way to escape this levirate remarriage, thus giving even greater social power to chastity. This policy, which seemed to many Chinese as incestuous, was quickly relaxed, but “a far reaching consequence was to import into Chinese law the steppe-inspired notion that a wife was, in a sense, purchased property” (Ropp 13).

From the 1260s to the 1320s, the inheritance laws were changed so that a widow’s in-laws gained control of her dowry and her remarriage. With these new laws put in place, widows were left extremely vulnerable to become martyrs for upholding Confucian values of chastity and loyalty to their husbands.

Under the new Ming dynasty, there was a sharp return to the emphasis on the five relationships. The five relationships of Confucianism are: between ruler and subject, father and son, elder brother and younger brother, husband and wife, and friend and friend. The relationships are in order of importance, placing a woman’s relationship with her husband under most others. With the Neoconfucian sentiment of society at the time, women who remained chaste, either by following their husbands to the grave or killing themselves to avoid rape, were held up as martrys and honored for their nobel deaths.
