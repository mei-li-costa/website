Widow Qian

The faithful Widow Qian became one of the first symbols of chastity after she committed suicide following the death of her husband in 1435. She was celebrated and honored in poetry by many and came to symbolize an honorable death for women. Widow fidelity was considered a Confucian teaching, however, before the Mongol conquest influence on marriage constructs and Widow Qian, it had been considered more of a precept, not a practice (Carlitz 612). The rise of Neoconfucian thought, however, paved the way to the glorification of a widow’s sacrifice.

Ji Chi, a famous poet, first established Widow Qian as a heroine of Confucian fidelity. He wrote about her to inspire his students and their wives to follow and emulate her commitment to Confucian values. The issue of chastity was framed with two choices, death or remarriage, the later being less honorable, enough to ruin a reputation of a family. Gong Xu, after Ji Chi, told similar stories and brought even more fame and praise to Window Qian’s sacrifice.

Widow Qian was “granted a shrine with the same spring and autumn sacrifices that the government accorded enshrined men” (Carlitz 612) for her suicide to follow her husband. She paved the way for more women to have shrines or arches erected in their names for the loyalty and chastity. These shrines, while similar in stature to men’s, were not celebrating men and women equally for intellectual strength. The “faithful heroine was enshrined for a gesture, rather than a life, and as an ideological construct, rather than a recognizable figure” (Carlitz 613).

References:

Carlitz, Katherine. “Shrines, Governing-Class Identity, and the Cult of Widow Fidelity in Mid-Ming Jiangnan.” The Journal of Asian Studies, vol. 56, no. 3, 1997, pp. 612–640. JSTOR, www.jstor.org/stable/2659603. Accessed 21 Apr. 2021.
