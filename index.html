Discussions of The Cult of Chastity

From Ming to Qing China

The Cult of Chastity or Widow Fidelity was a phenomenon that arose in the mid-Ming period with the rise neoconfucian values of a widow’s chastity. Following the invasion of the Mongols, China experienced a sharp sociocultural return to Confucian values, leaving space for the glorification of a widow’s sacrifice of her life to remain loyal to her husband. These women were honored and revered by the Chinese society at the time, marking one of women’s only moments of visibility in the public sphere.

Historial Perspectives on Female Suicide

"To starve to death is a minor thing; to lose one’s chastity is a great thing.”

Cheng Yi, 1033-1107

“She died well! She died well!”

Licentiate wang Yuhui, on his daughter's suicide in Rulin waishi by Wu Jingzi, 1701-1754

“If men want to make loyalty and righteousness their own responsibility, that is fine, but how does the virtue and chastity of women reflect any glory on men?”

Yu Zhenxie, 1775-1840

“These women [chaste widows] are to be pitied. Trapped for no good reason by tradition and numbers, they are sacrificed to no purpose.”

Lu Xun, 1881-1936
