Other Women Honored for Their Chastity

In Carlitz 1997 article on the female chastity and suicide in Ming China, she writes, ““Faithful women, excluded from public life while held up as exemplars to public officials, were perfect Others, on whom early Ming Confucian emotionalism could be projected” (Carlitz 617). These women were made into martyrs through the social climate that influence their strict adherence to chastity and the pressure to honor their family through suicide. The language used in society in Ming China shifted towards that which valued ultimate fidelity and with the erection of new shrines, women could not escape the societal influence. In the 1499 Changshu gazetteer, fifty women, of higher social class, were praised for their suicides.

In Jiangyin county, there is a recorded shrine that was created for Miss He. Miss He drown herself to avoid rape as she was fleeing warefare. The poet Wang Feng writes on her suicide, and proclaims, “how much better is it...that she dies to preserve her virtue and repay Heaven, than if she had simply returned home alive but dishonored!” (Carlitz 625).

The filial daughter Dou, who died long before Ming China, had her story placed into the new context of the times. Her shrine was rebuilt in 1470 and 1546 with an emphasis on the expression of the Five Relationships.

At a certain point in the Qing, the reward system for widows who committed suicide for chastity became so great that they had to make arches for collective groups of honorary women, instead of individual arches. By the Qing period, “female chastity was a metaphor for community honor” (Susan Mann 1987; Ropp 8).
